# Chapter 6 — A Neuro-Symbolic Reasoning Engine for Open-Vocabulary Transit Planning

This chapter presents the formal Knowledge Representation and Reasoning (KRR) basis of the NavLogic Bangkok system. The engine is constructed as a hybrid pipeline in which a stochastic neural component is responsible solely for the translation of free-form natural language into a strict logical surface, while all subsequent reasoning is carried out by a deterministic Prolog rulebook operating under the standard semantics of definite Horn-clause programs with negation as failure. We argue that this division of labour is not architecturally incidental but is required by the epistemic commitments of KRR: every deduction performed by the system must be inspectable, reproducible, and accompanied by a structural justification.

The remainder of the chapter develops this position in five sections. Section 6.1 fixes the formal contract between the neural and symbolic layers. Section 6.2 describes the Extensional Database (EDB) and the taxonomic subsumption hierarchy. Section 6.3 presents the goal evaluator as a meta-interpreter over an Abstract Syntax Tree (AST) produced by the LLM. Section 6.4 analyses constraint satisfaction as a generate-and-test procedure and motivates the placement of the cut operator. Section 6.5 develops the two feedback loops — relaxation and repair — as instances of systematic constraint weakening and diagnostic reasoning with symbolic constraint synthesis.

---

## 6.1 The Neuro-Symbolic Architecture

The system is structured as a three-stage pipeline:

1. A neural translator `T : Σ* → Φ` mapping a natural-language utterance over the input alphabet `Σ` to a well-formed logical formula `φ ∈ Φ` drawn from a fixed operator grammar.
2. A symbolic reasoner `R : Φ × KB → 2^P` mapping the formula and the knowledge base `KB` to the set of points of interest `P` satisfying it under the closed-world semantics of `KB`.
3. A neural narrator `N : Δ → Σ*` mapping a structured deductive trace `Δ` produced by `R` back to a natural-language explanation.

The translator `T` is implemented by a large language model (Google Gemini) constrained via function-calling to emit a term in a published operator grammar. The narrator `N` is the same model invoked in a separate context with a fixed structured input. Critically, `T` and `N` are the only stochastic components in the pipeline. The reasoner `R`, comprising goal evaluation, candidate generation, route construction, fiscal computation, audit, relaxation, and repair, is deterministic and exhibits the property that every deduction admits a syntactic proof tree.

This separation reflects a foundational KRR commitment: *neural systems are admissible at the boundary of representation, but reasoning over a representation must remain symbolic in order to preserve soundness, completeness, and explanatory adequacy.* The LLM is treated as a fallible interpretation function from a high-entropy domain (natural language) to a low-entropy formal surface; once the formal surface has been constructed, no further interpretation is permitted.

### 6.1.1 The Logical Surface

The formula language `Φ` is given by the following abstract grammar, with `t` ranging over a finite set of canonical tag atoms:

```
φ ::= and([φ, …, φ])
    | or([φ, …, φ])
    | not(φ)
    | any_tag([t, …, t])
    | all_tag([t, …, t])
    | none_tag([t, …, t])
    | route_to(StationAtom)
```

Each operator has fixed first-order semantics, given in Section 6.3. The grammar is closed under finite nesting; the LLM is taught by system prompt to emit only well-formed instances. An ill-formed term raises a `GoalShapeError` in the Python bridge layer prior to any Prolog invocation, ensuring that the symbolic reasoner is never presented with an utterance it cannot evaluate.

## 6.2 Knowledge Base and Ontology

### 6.2.1 The Extensional Database

The KB is partitioned into an Extensional Database (EDB) of ground unit clauses and an Intensional Database (IDB) of definite Horn clauses. The EDB consists of facts of the following forms:

```prolog
station(StationName, Line).
connects(StationA, StationB, Line).
edge(StationA, StationB, TravelTimeMinutes).
poi(PoiName, NearestStation, Description).
tagged(PoiName, Tag).
fare(Agency, TapInStation, TapOutStation, PriceTHB).
subtag(Specific, General).
synonym(Alias, CanonicalTag).
```

These constitute the ground atoms of the Herbrand base over which all subsequent deductions are performed. They are authored by hand or, in the case of `fare/4`, generated by a deterministic preprocessor (`scripts/build_fares.py`) from a tabular source. The EDB is monotonic: additions strictly increase the set of provable consequences and never invalidate prior derivations.

Of particular structural interest is the multiple declaration of interchange stations. The atom `'Siam (CEN)'` appears in two `station/2` clauses, one under the `bts_sukhumvit` line and one under `bts_silom`. This is not a redundancy: it is what makes `station_agency('Siam (CEN)', bts)` provable through either clause without a special case in the rule body, with the consequence that the Siam interchange is correctly fare-free (it never crosses an inter-agency boundary).

### 6.2.2 Taxonomic Subsumption as Transitive Closure

The ontology layer defines a partial order on tags via the directed acyclic graph induced by the binary `subtag/2` relation. Sample edges:

```prolog
subtag(buddhist_temple, temple).
subtag(chinese_temple,  temple).
subtag(hindu_shrine,    shrine).
subtag(temple,          cultural).
subtag(shrine,          cultural).
subtag(cultural,        sightseeing).
```

This DAG is intended to model `is_a` subsumption: a `buddhist_temple` is-a `temple`, a `temple` is-a `cultural` site, and so on. The full subsumption relation is the reflexive-transitive closure of `subtag/2`. It is computed by the recursive predicate `is_a/2`:

```prolog
is_a(X, X).                                  % reflexivity
is_a(X, Y) :- subtag(X, Y).                  % direct edge
is_a(X, Z) :- subtag(X, Y), is_a(Y, Z).      % transitivity
```

This is a textbook instance of computing reachability in a graph by recursive Horn-clause definition. Soundness with respect to the intended subsumption semantics is immediate; termination is guaranteed by the acyclicity of `subtag/2`. Operationally, SLD-resolution explores the subtag graph depth-first via successive unification with the second clause head, terminating either by reduction to a `subtag/2` ground fact (the second clause) or by reflexive identity (the first clause).

The ontology also provides a synonym layer:

```prolog
synonym(sweaty,   weather_exposed).
synonym(hot,      weather_exposed).
synonym(outdoors, weather_exposed).
```

Synonyms map non-canonical surface forms emitted by the LLM onto canonical tag atoms, prior to subsumption reasoning. The composite tag-resolution predicate `bind_tag/2` is presented in Section 6.4.

## 6.3 Goal Evaluation via Meta-Interpretation

### 6.3.1 The Meta-Interpreter

The predicate `satisfies/3` is a meta-interpreter in the standard sense: it is a Prolog program whose object-level argument is itself a term denoting a logical formula. The LLM emits an AST in the operator grammar of Section 6.1.1; `satisfies/3` recursively descends this AST, evaluating each operator according to its first-order semantics and delegating ground predicate evaluation to the EDB.

A simplified extract:

```prolog
satisfies(POI, and(Conjuncts), Ctx) :-
    forall(member(C, Conjuncts), satisfies(POI, C, Ctx)).

satisfies(POI, or(Disjuncts), Ctx) :-
    member(C, Disjuncts),
    satisfies(POI, C, Ctx).

satisfies(POI, not(G), Ctx) :-
    \+ satisfies(POI, G, Ctx).

satisfies(POI, any_tag(Tags), _) :-
    member(T, Tags),
    poi_has_tag(POI, T).

satisfies(POI, all_tag(Tags), _) :-
    forall(member(T, Tags), poi_has_tag(POI, T)).

satisfies(POI, none_tag(Tags), _) :-
    forall(member(T, Tags), \+ poi_has_tag(POI, T)).

poi_has_tag(POI, QueryTag) :-
    tagged(POI, ActualTag),
    is_a(ActualTag, QueryTag).
```

### 6.3.2 First-Order Semantics of the Operators

The semantics of each operator are precisely those of the corresponding first-order connectives over the Herbrand interpretation induced by the EDB:

- `and([φ₁, …, φₙ])` denotes `φ₁ ∧ … ∧ φₙ`, evaluated by `forall/2` over the conjunct list.
- `or([φ₁, …, φₙ])` denotes `φ₁ ∨ … ∨ φₙ`, evaluated by non-deterministic `member/2` selection followed by recursive evaluation, with backtracking exploring alternative disjuncts upon failure.
- `not(φ)` is implemented by `\+ satisfies(POI, φ, Ctx)`, which is **negation as failure** (NAF) in the sense of Clark.
- `any_tag([t₁, …, tₙ])` denotes `∃tᵢ . poi_has_tag(POI, tᵢ)`.
- `all_tag([t₁, …, tₙ])` denotes `∀tᵢ . poi_has_tag(POI, tᵢ)`.
- `none_tag([t₁, …, tₙ])` denotes `∀tᵢ . ¬poi_has_tag(POI, tᵢ)`, again realised through NAF.

### 6.3.3 The Closed World Assumption

The use of negation as failure presupposes the Closed World Assumption (CWA): any ground atom not derivable from the EDB augmented with the IDB is taken to be false. Within NavLogic, this assumption is justified by the curated nature of the EDB. The set of POIs, their tags, the taxonomy, and the fare table are exhaustively enumerated; absence of a fact is a positive assertion of negation, not a gap in knowledge. Under this regime, `none_tag([weather_exposed])` is sound: a POI fails to be classified `weather_exposed` precisely when no `tagged/2` clause and no transitive `is_a/2` derivation establishes it.

We note for completeness that the CWA fails in two well-defined regions: unrecognised tags emitted by the LLM (handled by the `unknown/1` wrapping mechanism of Section 6.4), and lines absent from the EDB but present in source data (the MRTA Pink/Purple/Yellow/Red lines, dropped at fare-table build time). Both regions are made explicit to the user rather than suppressed; the system never silently treats absence as falsity in domains where the EDB is known to be incomplete.

## 6.4 Constraint Satisfaction and State-Space Search

### 6.4.1 Generate-and-Test via `findall/3`

Candidate enumeration is implemented by the standard Prolog idiom of generate-and-test, expressed as:

```prolog
candidates(Goal, Cs) :-
    findall(POI,
            ( poi(POI, _, _),       % generator
              satisfies(POI, Goal, _) ),  % tester
            Cs).
```

The `poi/3` clause acts as a non-deterministic generator that, on backtracking, enumerates the entire POI set; `satisfies/3` acts as the tester that filters the stream by formula evaluation. `findall/3` collects the admissible bindings of `POI` into the list `Cs`, which is then returned to the orchestrator. This is a direct realisation of the generate-and-test paradigm and makes explicit that the candidate set is precisely the extension of the formula `Goal` over the POI domain under the semantics of Section 6.3.

### 6.4.2 The Cut Operator and Pruning of the Search Tree

Tag binding from a possibly unrecognised surface form to a canonical or wrapped representation is performed by `bind_tag/2`:

```prolog
bind_tag(Tag, Bound) :- tag(Tag),         !, Bound = Tag.        % (1)
bind_tag(Syn, Bound) :- synonym(Syn, Tag), !, Bound = Tag.       % (2)
bind_tag(Tag, unknown(Tag)).                                     % (3)
```

The cut operator (`!`) in clauses (1) and (2) is essential to the correctness of this predicate. Without the cuts, the SLD search tree for a known tag `Tag = temple` would proceed as follows. Clause (1) succeeds via `tag(temple)` and binds `Bound = temple`. Upon a subsequent backtracking request from the caller, the Prolog engine would attempt clause (3), which unconditionally succeeds with `Bound = unknown(temple)`. The query would therefore have two distinct solutions for a single recognised tag: `temple ; unknown(temple)`. This ambiguity propagates downstream: a single conjunct in a goal would non-deterministically resolve as both known and unknown, generating spurious candidate sets and incoherent unknown-tag reports.

The cuts in clauses (1) and (2) prevent this by **committing** to the corresponding clause once its precondition has been verified. Operationally, the cut prunes the search tree: it discards all alternative clauses for the current goal and all alternative choice points created since the most recent unification with the predicate's head. The intended semantics of `bind_tag/2` — known tags resolve to themselves, synonyms to their canonical form, and only unrecognised tags are wrapped as `unknown/1` — is thereby restored as the unique declarative reading.

We note that this is an instance of a **soft cut**: the third clause is reachable only when the preconditions of both prior clauses fail, behaving as a default. The construct is locally read as if-then-else and is the canonical Prolog technique for encoding ordered defaults in the absence of explicit conditional syntax.

### 6.4.3 Bounded State-Space Search

Beyond candidate enumeration, the orchestrator performs shortest-path search over the station graph using Dijkstra's algorithm. The graph is constructed in Python from the `edge/3` facts of the EDB, but the construction is purely numerical and lies outside the scope of symbolic reasoning. We emphasise that the placement of Dijkstra outside Prolog is a deliberate design decision: shortest-path computation over weighted graphs is well-served by imperative implementations, whereas the symbolic layer is reserved for the rule-laden tasks of subsumption, satisfaction, and audit.

## 6.5 Symbolic Repair and Constraint Weakening

### 6.5.1 Relaxation as Systematic Constraint Weakening

When `candidates/3` returns the empty set, the orchestrator invokes the relaxation predicate to compute a minimal weakening of the goal that admits at least one candidate. The predicate is defined schematically as:

```prolog
relax(and(Conjuncts), Dropped, RelaxedGoal, Survivors) :-
    select(Dropped, Conjuncts, Remaining),
    RelaxedGoal = and(Remaining),
    candidates(RelaxedGoal, Survivors),
    Survivors \= [].
```

This implements **single-conjunct constraint weakening**: the predicate non-deterministically selects one conjunct to remove, constructs the residual conjunction, and tests whether the relaxed formula admits any candidates. Backtracking enumerates all single-conjunct relaxations; the `Survivors \= []` constraint filters out unproductive drops. If no single-conjunct relaxation succeeds, the orchestrator iteratively requests two-conjunct, three-conjunct, and so forth, instantiating a complete (though monotonically weakening) relaxation cascade.

This procedure is an instance of the classical KRR notion of **belief contraction by minimal mutilation**: the original constraint set is weakened to its largest admissible subset, with the dropped conjunct surfaced to the user as part of the explanatory output. The relaxation respects priority by virtue of evaluation order; richer schemes (e.g. weighted relaxation) would extend `select/3` to a priority-ordered selector but are not implemented in the present system.

### 6.5.2 Diagnostic Reasoning and the Audit Loop

The audit predicate `audit_route/3` performs **diagnostic reasoning** over a candidate route. Whereas `satisfies/3` evaluates a formula against a destination, `audit_route/3` evaluates it against a *path* — specifically, the sequence of ride and transfer steps generated by Dijkstra. The predicate succeeds with a structured `Violation` term when any step contradicts a constraint of the formula:

```prolog
audit_route(Goal, Route, Violation) :-
    member(Step, Route),
    step_violates(Goal, Step, Violation).

audit_route(_, _, clean).
```

When a violation is detected, the orchestrator blacklists the offending POI and re-enters `candidates/3` to select the next-preferred admissible candidate. The loop is bounded by a constant `MAX_REPLAN` to ensure termination in the presence of pathological inputs. The diagnostic character of this loop is essential: the symbolic layer does not merely filter inputs to the numeric search but **vetoes its outputs** when those outputs, despite reaching admissible destinations, traverse paths that contradict the formula.

### 6.5.3 Symbolic Synthesis: The Repair Loop

The fiscal repair loop is the centrepiece KRR contribution of the system. When a Dijkstra-optimal route exceeds a user-pinned monetary budget, Prolog performs a structured diagnosis:

```prolog
diagnose_budget(trip_fare(Total, Segs), cap(Cap),
                over_budget(Total, Overage, Segs, Bounds)) :-
    Total > Cap,
    Overage is Total - Cap,
    path_boundaries(Segs, Bounds).
```

The diagnosis is not a Boolean: it is a structured term carrying the total cost, the overage, the agency-partitioned segments, and the inter-agency boundaries crossed. This structure is then consumed by `propose_repair/3`, which **synthesises a new ground constraint** intended to bound the next iteration of the numeric search:

```prolog
propose_repair(over_budget(_, _, Segs, Bounds), Tried,
               avoid_specific_boundary(A, B)) :-
    rank_boundaries(Bounds, Segs, Ranked),
    member(boundary(A, B, _, _), Ranked),
    \+ member(avoid_specific_boundary(A, B), Tried).

propose_repair(over_budget(_, _, _, Bounds), Tried,
               avoid_agency_pair(AA, AB)) :-
    member(boundary(_, _, AA, AB), Bounds),
    \+ member(avoid_agency_pair(AA, AB), Tried).

propose_repair(over_budget(_, _, Segs, _), Tried,
               force_single_agency(A)) :-
    member(seg(A, _, _), Segs),
    \+ member(force_single_agency(A), Tried).

propose_repair(over_budget(Total, _, _, _), _,
               infeasible(structurally_over_budget(Total))).
```

Four clauses define a strictly weaker cascade of repair tiers: pruning a single boundary, pruning all boundaries between an agency pair, restricting the path to a single agency, and finally certifying infeasibility. The `Tried` accumulator implements memoisation, preventing infinite cycling on a previously-attempted repair. Each successful repair emits a ground term that did not appear in the EDB prior to the failure: it is a constraint **synthesised at runtime in response to a deductive failure**.

The synthesised constraint is then **reflected into the numeric search**: the Python procedure `_build_graph_with_pruning` consumes the repair term and removes the corresponding edges from the Dijkstra graph. Numeric search proceeds on a graph that the symbolic layer has shaped. The semantic loop is closed:

1. Numeric search proposes a path.
2. Symbolic reasoning diagnoses why the path violates the budget.
3. Symbolic reasoning synthesises a new constraint.
4. The constraint is reflected into the numeric search space.
5. Numeric search retries on a pruned graph.

Bounded by a constant `MAX_REPAIRS`, the cascade terminates either with a path satisfying the budget or with the emission of a **constructive infeasibility certificate** by the predicate `explain_infeasibility/3`:

```prolog
Certificate = certificate(
    repairs_exhausted([
        step(avoid_specific_boundary(A, B), re_routed_total(T₁)),
        step(avoid_agency_pair(AA, AB),     graph_disconnected),
        step(force_single_agency(A),        graph_disconnected)
    ]),
    final_over_by(Δ),
    min_seen(M)
).
```

The certificate is a syntactic proof of infeasibility: it enumerates the repair attempts, records the outcome of each, and reports the minimum achievable cost. From a KRR standpoint, this is a **constructive negative answer** — the system does not merely report that no path was found, but provides the deductive witness that no path exists under the conjunction of the user's preferences and the structural properties of the network. This is precisely the form of explanation that distinguishes a reasoning system from a search procedure with a failure return value.

### 6.5.4 Structural Parallelism of the Two Loops

The audit loop of Section 6.5.2 and the repair loop of Section 6.5.3 are structurally parallel. Both consist of:

1. A numeric search proposing an artefact (a route, a path).
2. A symbolic diagnosis identifying a violation (a forbidden tag on a transfer; a budget overage with a structured boundary breakdown).
3. A symbolic synthesis of a constraint to exclude the violating artefact from subsequent search (POI blacklisting; edge or agency-pair pruning).
4. A bounded retry of the numeric search under the augmented constraint set.
5. A constructive failure certificate when the retry budget is exhausted.

The audit loop blacklists destinations; the repair loop blacklists graph edges. Both are instances of the more general schema *"deduce a failure from a numeric search space, synthesise a ground constraint that excludes the failure, and retry within a bounded budget."* It is this schema that justifies the description of the system as performing **symbolic supervision of numeric search** rather than the more limited claim of symbolic post-filtering.

## 6.6 Worked Example: An End-to-End Reasoning Trace

To make the preceding mechanisms concrete, we present a complete trace of one user query through the engine, annotating each step with the formal device of the previous sections that it instantiates. We then extend the trace under a tightened monetary budget to exercise the repair loop of Section 6.5.3.

### 6.6.1 Query and Translation

Consider the natural-language input:

> *"I want to visit a temple, but I hate the heat."*

with conversational context establishing the origin as `'Asok (E4)'`. The translator `T` of Section 6.1, executed by the LLM under function-call constraints, emits the structured term:

```
{ origin: "Asok",
  goal:   { op: "and",
            args: [ { op: "any_tag",  tags: ["temple"] },
                    { op: "none_tag", tags: ["weather_exposed"] } ] } }
```

The Python bridge layer validates the operator grammar of Section 6.1.1 and constructs the corresponding Prolog term:

```prolog
Goal = and([ any_tag([temple]),
             none_tag([weather_exposed]) ]).
```

This term is the AST that the meta-interpreter `satisfies/3` will subsequently descend. No further interpretive activity is performed by the LLM until the final narration step.

### 6.6.2 Tag Binding and the Cut

Both tag atoms `temple` and `weather_exposed` are first passed through `bind_tag/2`. For `temple`, clause (1) of Section 6.4.2 is selected; the precondition `tag(temple)` succeeds against the EDB; the cut commits to clause (1); and the binding `Bound = temple` is returned. The alternative clause (3), which would have produced the spurious binding `Bound = unknown(temple)`, is excluded from the search tree by the cut. This is the operational effect described in Section 6.4.2: the cut enforces the ordered-default semantics of `bind_tag/2`, ensuring that a recognised tag is never reinterpreted as unknown on backtracking. The same procedure applies to `weather_exposed`.

### 6.6.3 Candidate Generation by Generate-and-Test

The orchestrator issues:

```prolog
?- candidates(and([any_tag([temple]),
                   none_tag([weather_exposed])]), Cs).
```

By the definition of Section 6.4.1, `findall/3` enumerates the POI domain through the generator `poi(POI, _, _)` and tests each binding by recursive evaluation of `satisfies/3`. We trace the three relevant POIs.

**POI 1: `'Wat Pho'`.** The first conjunct, `any_tag([temple])`, succeeds via the following SLD derivation:

```
satisfies('Wat Pho', any_tag([temple]), _)
  ⟶  member(T, [temple]) ∧ poi_has_tag('Wat Pho', T)
  ⟶  T = temple ∧ poi_has_tag('Wat Pho', temple)
  ⟶  tagged('Wat Pho', Actual) ∧ is_a(Actual, temple)
  ⟶  Actual = buddhist_temple ∧ is_a(buddhist_temple, temple)
```

The subgoal `is_a(buddhist_temple, temple)` is resolved by the second clause of `is_a/2`, reducing to `subtag(buddhist_temple, temple)`, which is a ground EDB fact. This is the transitive-closure mechanism of Section 6.2.2 in operation: subsumption is derived rather than enumerated.

The second conjunct, `none_tag([weather_exposed])`, fails. By the third clause of `satisfies/3`:

```
satisfies('Wat Pho', none_tag([weather_exposed]), _)
  ⟶  forall(member(T, [weather_exposed]),
             ¬poi_has_tag('Wat Pho', T))
  ⟶  ¬poi_has_tag('Wat Pho', weather_exposed)
```

The negation-as-failure subgoal requires that `poi_has_tag('Wat Pho', weather_exposed)` be unprovable. The Prolog engine attempts the proof:

```
poi_has_tag('Wat Pho', weather_exposed)
  ⟶  tagged('Wat Pho', Actual) ∧ is_a(Actual, weather_exposed)
```

The first matching `tagged/2` clause yields `Actual = buddhist_temple`; `is_a(buddhist_temple, weather_exposed)` is unprovable and the engine backtracks. The second matching clause yields `Actual = weather_exposed`; the reflexive clause `is_a(X, X)` immediately succeeds. The atom `poi_has_tag('Wat Pho', weather_exposed)` is therefore provable. By the definition of negation as failure, `¬poi_has_tag('Wat Pho', weather_exposed)` fails, the surrounding `forall/2` fails, the second conjunct of the goal fails, the conjunction fails, and `'Wat Pho'` is excluded from the candidate set.

This step is a direct realisation of the closed-world assumption discussed in Section 6.3.3: the EDB explicitly asserts `tagged('Wat Pho', weather_exposed)`, the assertion is reachable through the subsumption hierarchy, and the negation is therefore correctly resolved as false.

**POI 2: `'Wat Mangkon'`.** The first conjunct succeeds analogously to POI 1, with the derivation passing through `tagged('Wat Mangkon', chinese_temple)` and `subtag(chinese_temple, temple)`. For the second conjunct, the proof attempt of `poi_has_tag('Wat Mangkon', weather_exposed)` exhausts all `tagged/2` matches without producing a derivation; the negation succeeds; the conjunction succeeds; and `'Wat Mangkon'` is admitted to the candidate set.

**POI 3: `'Erawan Shrine'`.** Both conjuncts succeed by analogous derivations, the first via the subsumption chain `hindu_shrine → shrine → cultural` (subject to the chosen interpretation of `temple` in the ontology), and the second by the absence of any `weather_exposed` derivation for this POI. `'Erawan Shrine'` is admitted.

The result returned to the orchestrator is:

```prolog
Cs = ['Wat Mangkon', 'Erawan Shrine'].
```

### 6.6.4 Numeric Search and the Audit Loop

The orchestrator constructs a weighted directed graph from the `edge/3` facts of the EDB and applies Dijkstra's algorithm to compute a minimum-time path to each candidate's nearest station. The procedure is purely numeric and is not part of the symbolic system; we record only its outputs:

```
Route_Erawan      = [ ride(bts_sukhumvit, 'Asok (E4)', 'Chit Lom (E1)', 3),
                      transfer('Chit Lom (E1)', 'Erawan Shrine') ]
Route_WatMangkon  = [ ride(bts_sukhumvit, 'Asok (E4)', 'Siam (CEN)', 3),
                      transfer('Siam (CEN)', 'Siam (CEN)'),
                      ride(bts_silom,     'Siam (CEN)', 'Sala Daeng (S2)', 1),
                      transfer('Sala Daeng (S2)', 'Silom (BL26)'),
                      ride(mrt_blue,      'Silom (BL26)', 'Wat Mangkon (BL29)', 3) ]
```

The orchestrator selects `'Erawan Shrine'` as the preferred candidate by minimum total time and submits its route to the audit predicate of Section 6.5.2:

```prolog
?- audit_route(and([any_tag([temple]),
                    none_tag([weather_exposed])]),
               Route_Erawan, Violation).
```

`audit_route/3` enumerates the steps of the route via `member/2`. The first step is a ride between Asok and Chit Lom; no `step_violates/3` derivation succeeds for it. The second step is the transfer `transfer('Chit Lom (E1)', 'Erawan Shrine')`. The auxiliary fact `transfer_property('Chit Lom (E1)', 'Erawan Shrine', weather_exposed)` is derivable from the EDB. Pattern-matching the goal yields the conjunct `none_tag([weather_exposed])`, which forbids `weather_exposed`. The predicate succeeds with:

```prolog
Violation = transfer_violates(weather_exposed,
                              'Chit Lom (E1)',
                              'Erawan Shrine').
```

This is a structured diagnostic term in the sense of Section 6.5.2: the symbolic layer has identified not merely that the route is inadmissible but precisely *why*, and at *which step*. The orchestrator adds `'Erawan Shrine'` to a blacklist, decrements the bound `MAX_REPLAN`, and re-enters the audit loop with the next candidate. The audit of `Route_WatMangkon` enumerates all five steps without producing a `step_violates/3` derivation; the fall-through clause `audit_route(_, _, clean)` succeeds; and `'Wat Mangkon'` is accepted as the final result. The audit trail recorded in the response is:

```
audit_trail = [ rejected('Erawan Shrine',
                         transfer_violates(weather_exposed,
                                           'Chit Lom (E1)',
                                           'Erawan Shrine')) ]
```

### 6.6.5 Narration

The orchestrator assembles a structured response containing the origin, the destination, the route steps, the audit trail, and any relaxation or unknown-tag annotations (here both empty). The narrator `N` of Section 6.1 is invoked with this structure as its input. The natural-language output produced by `N` is constrained to faithfully report the structured fields; no creative inference is performed. A typical narration is:

> *"Wat Mangkon Kamalawat is reachable in approximately 22 minutes via the BTS Sukhumvit, BTS Silom, and MRT Blue lines, with all transfers fully indoor. The Erawan Shrine was considered but rejected because the transfer from Chit Lom to the shrine traverses an open-air walkway, which conflicts with your stated preference."*

### 6.6.6 Trace Extension: Triggering the Repair Loop

To exercise the repair loop of Section 6.5.3, we now consider a second query under tightened budget conditions:

> *"I have a budget of ฿30. Where can I go that isn't crowded? Starting from Phaya Thai."*

The translator emits:

```
{ origin: "Phaya Thai",
  goal:           { op: "none_tag", tags: ["crowded"] },
  budget_context: { max_thb: 30 } }
```

The orchestrator binds `Budget = cap(30)` and threads it parallel to the goal, in accordance with the architectural commitment of Section 6.5.3 that monetary constraints are not relaxable conjuncts. Candidate enumeration proceeds as in Section 6.6.3 and yields a list including `'Wat Suthat'` (nearest station `'Sam Yot (BL30)'`).

For this candidate, Dijkstra produces a station path that traverses BTS Sukhumvit and BTS Silom from Phaya Thai to Sala Daeng, transfers to MRT Blue at Silom, and continues to Sam Yot. The fiscal layer is invoked:

```prolog
?- trip_fare(['Phaya Thai (N2)', …, 'Sam Yot (BL30)'], Origin, Fare).
```

`path_segments/2` partitions the path into agency-homogeneous runs. Greedy extension under `station_agency/2` produces:

```prolog
Segments = [ seg(bts, 'Phaya Thai (N2)', 'Sala Daeng (S2)'),
             seg(bem, 'Silom (BL26)',    'Sam Yot (BL30)') ].
```

We note that `'Siam (CEN)'`, traversed mid-path, remains within the BTS segment because it is declared under both `bts_sukhumvit` and `bts_silom` in `station/2`; `station_agency('Siam (CEN)', bts)` is provable and the segment is not closed. This is the structural property identified in Section 6.2.1 and is essential to the correctness of the partition. Per-segment fares are looked up from `fare/4`:

```
fare(bts, 'Phaya Thai (N2)', 'Sala Daeng (S2)', 44).
fare(bem, 'Silom (BL26)',    'Sam Yot (BL30)',   29).
```

`trip_fare/3` returns `Fare = trip_fare(73, [...])`. The diagnostic predicate is invoked:

```prolog
?- diagnose_budget(trip_fare(73, Segs), cap(30), Diagnosis).
```

The first clause of `diagnose_budget/3` requires `73 ≤ 30` and fails. The second clause succeeds, computing `Overage = 43` and extracting the inter-agency boundaries:

```prolog
Diagnosis = over_budget(
    73, 43,
    [ seg(bts, 'Phaya Thai (N2)', 'Sala Daeng (S2)'),
      seg(bem, 'Silom (BL26)',    'Sam Yot (BL30)') ],
    [ boundary('Sala Daeng (S2)', 'Silom (BL26)', bts, bem) ]
).
```

This is the structured failure term of Section 6.5.3. The orchestrator passes it to `propose_repair/3` with `Tried = []`. The first clause matches: `rank_boundaries/3` orders the single boundary by `boundary_cost/3` and the predicate emits:

```prolog
Repair = avoid_specific_boundary('Sala Daeng (S2)', 'Silom (BL26)').
```

This is the runtime symbolic synthesis of a ground constraint that did not appear in the EDB prior to this derivation. The constraint is reflected into the numeric layer by `_build_graph_with_pruning`, which removes the corresponding edge from a copy of the Dijkstra graph. Numeric search is re-executed on the pruned graph; an alternate path is computed, partitioned, and priced. The new total is, hypothetically, ฿40. `diagnose_budget/3` is re-invoked; the over-budget clause again succeeds, with `Overage = 10`. `propose_repair/3` is invoked with `Tried = [avoid_specific_boundary('Sala Daeng (S2)', 'Silom (BL26)')]`. The first clause is now blocked by the memoisation guard `\+ member(...)`, and the second clause fires:

```prolog
Repair = avoid_agency_pair(bts, bem).
```

This constraint, semantically weaker than its predecessor, is reflected into the graph by removing all BTS↔MRT transfer edges. The pruned graph is now disconnected with respect to the candidate destination. Dijkstra returns no path. The orchestrator records the disconnection. `propose_repair/3` is invoked once more; the third clause fires:

```prolog
Repair = force_single_agency(bts).
```

The graph is re-pruned to BTS-only edges; again no path exists. The bound `MAX_REPAIRS` is now reached. `explain_infeasibility/3` is invoked and emits the constructive infeasibility certificate:

```prolog
Certificate = certificate(
    repairs_exhausted([
        step(avoid_specific_boundary('Sala Daeng (S2)', 'Silom (BL26)'),
             re_routed_total(40)),
        step(avoid_agency_pair(bts, bem), graph_disconnected),
        step(force_single_agency(bts),    graph_disconnected)
    ]),
    final_over_by(10),
    min_seen(40)
).
```

The certificate constitutes a syntactic proof that, under the conjunction of the user's tag preferences, the user's monetary budget, the structure of the network, and the bounded repair cascade, no admissible route exists. The orchestrator surfaces the certificate to the narrator, which produces a faithful natural-language explanation:

> *"Wat Suthat cannot be reached from Phaya Thai under a ฿30 budget. The cheapest single-boundary repair re-routes via an alternate transfer for ฿40, still ฿10 over. Banning all BTS↔MRT crossings disconnects the destination, as does restricting to BTS only. The minimum achievable fare under your constraints is ฿40."*

The same procedure is then applied to the next candidate in preference order. If no candidate produces an admissible route, the response carries the certificates for each in the `budget_audit` field, again in the spirit of Section 6.5.3: the absence of a route is reported as a deductive consequence, not as an absence of information.

### 6.6.7 Theoretical Recapitulation of the Trace

## 6.7 Summary

The NavLogic Bangkok engine instantiates a strict separation between stochastic semantic extraction at the system boundary and deterministic symbolic reasoning at the system core. Knowledge is represented as a set of ground unit clauses (the EDB) and a small body of recursive Horn clauses (the IDB). Subsumption is computed as the reflexive-transitive closure of a `subtag/2` DAG. Goal evaluation is realised as a meta-interpreter over an LLM-generated AST in a fixed operator grammar, with operator semantics drawn from first-order logic and negation realised as failure under the closed-world assumption. Constraint satisfaction proceeds by generate-and-test using `findall/3`, with cut operators placed to prune the search tree and enforce an ordered-default reading of tag binding. When the constraint set admits no candidate, the engine performs systematic constraint weakening; when a numerically optimal artefact violates a constraint, the engine performs diagnostic reasoning and synthesises new ground constraints to bound the next iteration of search. When all repair attempts fail, the engine emits a constructive infeasibility certificate.

Each of these mechanisms maps directly onto a foundational concept of KRR: ground atoms in the Herbrand base; transitive closure of binary relations; Horn-clause SLD-resolution; meta-interpretation of object-level formulas; negation as failure under the CWA; cut and the pruning of choice points; generate-and-test as a search paradigm; minimal-mutilation belief contraction; diagnostic reasoning over structured failures; and runtime synthesis of ground constraints with reflection into a numeric search space. The thesis defended by this construction is that an end-to-end natural-language transit assistant can be built such that *every* deduction the system performs admits a syntactic proof, and that the neural component is restricted to a pure translation role at the system boundary. The engine's behaviour is, in its symbolic core, a model-theoretic consequence of its rulebook.
